Active Dynamic Load Adaptation for Quadruped Locomotion on Complex Terrain

Anonymous

Abstract

Quadruped robots show significant potential for load-carrying tasks due to their terrain adaptability. A unique challenge of these tasks is that the loads can move actively, and their mass and center of mass change dynamically, rather than being integrated as a whole-body component of the quadruped. Unlike traditional load-carrying tasks, where the load is typically passive and its influence on the robot's movement is predictable and static, active dynamic loads can actively alter the robot's balance control in real-time, posing load disturbances to locomotion. These load disturbances, when combined with the fundamental attitude changes induced by complex terrain, create dual dynamic disturbances for the robot. To address these dual disturbances, we propose an active dynamic load modeling approach that captures the active and dynamic characteristics of the load, enabling the robot to adapt to the real-time changes in load movement. This approach is integrated into a Reinforcement Learning (RL) framework that leverages dynamic models: an Inverse Dynamic Model (IDM) that learns the dynamic characteristics of the active load, and a Forward Dynamic Model (FDM) that predicts the effects of complex terrain on the robot's motion, enabling synchronous adaptation to both types of dynamic disturbances. Extensive comparative simulations and physical experiments across diverse terrains, with active dynamic loads of varying movements, demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in enhancing balance control and adaptability.

Method

We propose an end-to-end reinforcement learning framework that enables quadruped robots to adapt to active dynamic loads and complex terrain using only proprioceptive inputs. The method includes three cooperative modules: an Inverse Dynamics Module (IDM) that infers active load-induced forces and estimates load state, a Forward Dynamics Module (FDM) that models terrain-induced dynamics and predicts latent state transitions, and an Actor-Critic policy trained with PPO that consumes proprioception along with learned latent embeddings from both dynamics modules to produce robust joint torques. The IDM learns to infer load characteristics (mass, CoM, joint states) from observation histories, while the FDM trains an environment encoder to capture terrain disturbances. These latent embeddings are concatenated with proprioceptive observations and fed to the actor network; the critic has access to privileged information during training. The combined architecture enables synchronous adaptation to both load and terrain disturbances.

Simulation Experiments

We evaluate stability and adaptability under various terrains (flat, stairs, rough, slope) with an active robotic-arm load producing motion patterns such as front-back, left-right, rotation and random.
